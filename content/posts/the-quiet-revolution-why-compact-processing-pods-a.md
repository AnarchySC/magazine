## The Quiet Revolution: Why Compact Processing Pods Are Reshaping the Galaxy

Somewhere between Computex Orbital last cycle and now, something shifted. Walk any fabrication market from Titan Station to the Kessel Corridor and you'll notice it: the big tower processing pods — the kind that used to dominate the DIY assembly scene — are gathering dust. What's moving is the small stuff. Micro-shell and mini-shell builds. Compact chassis. Pods you could strap to a bulkhead and forget about.

I wanted to understand *why*, so I cornered be quiet!'s CEO, Aaron Licht, at their booth during the Orbital Expo. He builds his own systems. That's the first thing he told me, unprompted. I liked him immediately.

"The mini-ITX boom — we're calling it that internally, though honestly 'boom' undersells it," Licht said, adjusting a prototype chassis that sat on the table between us like a very calm metal sandwich. "Three cycles ago, compact builds were enthusiast projects. Edge cases. Now they're the center of the conversation."

The interesting part isn't that the trend exists. It's *why* it exists.

**Space is the point.** Station habitats — and I'm talking about the ones ordinary people actually live in, not the Assembly councillors' palatial docking suites — are getting smaller. Credit dilution, housing compression, the slow squeeze of corporate colony management: your living quarter is 40% smaller than your grandparent's. Your processing pod needs to fit inside your life, not the other way around.

Licht walked me through be quiet!'s new Pure Base 803 line — available in both standard and compact shell formats — and what struck me wasn't the industrial design (though the magnetic-latch dust filtration is genuinely elegant, and I'll have a schematic for you at the bottom of this piece). What struck me was the *philosophy* embedded in the build.

"We designed for repairability first," he said. That phrase. I wrote it down. "Every panel comes off with two screws or less. The cable routing is documented. The cooling architecture is published."

Published. Openly. I asked him why.

"I don't understand the question. Why wouldn't I share it?"

I'm going to be honest: I had to compose myself.

The Pure Base 803 compact variant fits processing cores up to the current Psion-class architecture with room to spare — and more importantly, room to breathe. The crossflow thermal design handles up to 280W continuous dissipation in a 26-liter envelope. Here's how you can try this yourself: the full thermal measurement methodology is open on be quiet!'s fabrication repository. Pull it. Replicate it. Tell me if you get different numbers.

But let's talk about the PSU calculator, because this is where it gets interesting.

be quiet! built a free, open-access power consumption estimator — you input your components, it outputs draw estimates. Sounds trivial. It's not. The mega-corps like StellarRig have been selling pre-configured pods with oversized, overpriced power units for cycles now, bundled at mandatory margins that inflate the build cost by 15-20%. A tool that tells you exactly what you need breaks that scam cleanly.

"We lose some PSU sales when people realize they needed the 650W unit, not the 1200W unit we could have sold them," Licht said. "That's fine."

They patented the *thermal simulation model* anyway — some licensing agreement with a components conglomerate I'd rather not name — but the calculator itself remains free. It's the compromise you make when you're not running a worker collective out of an asteroid. I'm not going to pretend the galaxy runs on pure gift economy. Not yet.

The compact processing pod revolution is, at its core, a response to compression. Less space, less credit, more ingenuity. The builders driving this market are the same people who figured out how to run full fabrication labs in two-room colony apartments. They are, as they have always been, ahead of everyone.

be quiet! is meeting them where they are. That's rarer than it should be.
