# The End of Industrial Food: Only Regenerative Farming Can Feed the Galaxy

**By 松田 ジェイド | Books & Media Critic, Cassette Future Magazine**

---

Korvus Tane gets asked the same question everywhere he goes. At the Frontier Settlement Agronomy Symposium on Callisto. At the underground food markets of the Kessel Corridor. In the comment threads of every independent transmission he's ever published.

*Can regenerative farming actually feed the galaxy?*

His answer, delivered with the exhausted patience of a man who has said it ten thousand times, is always the same:

**It's the only thing that ever has.**

---

Tane's new lecture series, *Soil Before Stars*, recently compiled into a distributed-processing core archive, isn't really about farming. I want to say that clearly, because most people who dismiss him think it is. They hear 'composting' and 'natural soil cycles' and 'closed-loop nutrient systems' and they stop listening.

But what is it actually saying?

The surface argument is about food production. The real argument is about whether industrial civilization knows what it destroyed when it scaled up — and whether it has the honesty to admit the cost.

Tane's central claim is devastating in its simplicity: five hundred years ago, before the great Galactic Harvest Maximization Programs, frontier settlements and Earth's own pre-industrial regions fed more people per hectare of living soil than they do today. Not in spite of 'primitive' methods. *Because* of them. Regenerative agriculture — rotating crops, integrating animals, building fungal soil networks, refusing synthetic chemical intervention — doesn't just maintain land. It *generates* it. Every season, the soil deepens. Every year, the ecosystem becomes more resilient.

Industrial agri-corps do the opposite. Every season, the substrate degrades. Every year, you need more chemical input to extract the same yield. You are not farming the land. You are *mining* it.

---

Look at what the ending asks us to accept.

The Galactic Harvest Maximization Programs — championed across the last century by mega-corps like NutriStar Consolidated and Apex Agro Solutions — promised abundance through scale. Monocultures across entire colony moons. Synthetic fertilizers manufactured in orbital processing stations. Pesticide arrays delivered by automated drone fleets. The numbers looked extraordinary in the short term. Assembly nutritional reports glowed. Quarterly yields on the Ceres Exchange soared.

And now? The frontier soil surveys from 2930-2934 are not comfortable reading. Seventeen colony settlements report substrate collapse. Six more report yield reductions exceeding forty percent. The synthetic fertilizer dependency loop has become so complete that any supply chain disruption — and we've had several — translates directly into starvation risk.

The villain in this story believes efficiency is the highest value. Believes that if you can extract more today, you should. Believes soil is a substrate, not a system. And the Assembly's agricultural policy agrees more than it admits.

---

Tane is not a romantic. He doesn't dress in ancestral fabric and talk about the Earth Mother. He is, frankly, kind of rude about people who treat regenerative farming as a spiritual lifestyle choice rather than applied ecology.

*'This isn't philosophy,'* he says in one of the archive segments, sounding genuinely annoyed. *'This is basic systems thinking. A system that consumes faster than it regenerates will collapse. This isn't controversial. This is thermodynamics. We just decided, for a century, to ignore it because the quarterly reports looked good.'*

This is a story about what it means to mistake extraction for productivity. To look at a depleting asset and call it growth. To inherit ten thousand years of accumulated soil wisdom from pre-spaceflight Earth and Frontier Settlement indigenous practice, and discard it because it couldn't be patented.

---

NutriStar Consolidated did not respond to requests for comment on their substrate degradation surveys. Apex Agro Solutions' neural-feed press account posted, in the same week Tane's archive was released, a seventeen-image carousel about their 'commitment to sustainable yield innovation.'

It did not define 'sustainable.'

The archive, for anyone who wants it, is available on every independent transmission node in the Frontier Settlements. It has been downloaded eleven million times in six weeks.

Somebody is listening.

The soil, if we let it, already knows how to answer the question.
