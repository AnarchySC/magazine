## Solar Defense Compact Opens 'Community Launch Pad' for Small Operators — Read the Fine Print

Here is a thing that happened: the Solar Defense Compact issued a public solicitation last week advertising **Launch Corridor Nine** at Helios Station Seven — previously a restricted military launch facility — as newly available to small and medium independent rocket operators.

The press release used the words *opportunity*, *partnership*, and *community access* four times each. I counted.

Let's talk about what's actually being offered.

---

### What the Solicitation Says

The SDC is seeking 'potential users' for a newly designated commercial launch corridor. Small lift capacity — roughly 500kg to low-solar orbit. Medium lift up to about 4,000kg. Slots would be available on a scheduling basis, coordinated through — and here it is — **SDC Launch Operations Command**.

Not an independent port authority. Not a neutral civilian transit body. The military. Scheduling your launch. On their calendar.

The solicitation is careful not to use the word *approval*. It says *coordination*. These are not the same word, but in practice, at a facility where the surrounding corridors are still classified restricted space, they function identically.

Here's how you can try this yourself: read the solicitation document (public record, linked below in this article's open archive). Count how many times the phrase *subject to operational requirements* appears. I found it nine times in four pages. Every access guarantee is downstream of that phrase.

---

### Why Helios Station Seven?

The SDC has operated Helios Seven primarily as a test range for the last eleven years. Specifically — and this is documented in the Interstellar Assembly's own budget disclosures — it has been used for **propulsion stress-testing** related to the Javelin IV long-range interceptor program.

The interesting part isn't that they're offering space. It's *why* they're offering it *now*.

Helios Seven's eastern launch corridors were mothballed in 2931 when the Javelin IV program shifted testing to the Kuiper Auxiliary Range. That left the SDC holding a large, expensive facility with active staff, maintained ground systems, and a fixed operational budget — generating nothing.

The 'community access' announcement is, in structural terms, a cost-sharing exercise. Which is fine. Cost-sharing is rational. But it is not philanthropy, and it is not infrastructure policy. It is a military base looking for tenants to help pay the power bill.

---

### The Access Problem Nobody Is Naming

Independent launch operators — the small courier outfits, the research collectives, the materials-testing co-ops — have been asking the Core Systems Colony Administration for neutral civilian launch infrastructure for *years*. The bottleneck for small operators isn't rocket technology. Fabrication has solved most of that. The bottleneck is *ground access*.

Every major launch facility in the inner solar system is either SDC-operated, leased to a mega-corp, or both. Cygnus Orbital charges docking and corridor fees that consume roughly 34% of a small operator's per-launch margin before they've bought a gram of propellant. I have those numbers from three independent co-ops who gave me permission to publish them. They're in the open archive.

Offering a military range as a 'solution' to the civilian access problem is — I'm trying to find a diplomatic way to say this — the problem wearing a different hat.

---

### What Would Actually Help

A neutral, community-governed launch commons. Managed by operators. Fees set by operators. Scheduling run by a transparent, auditable, non-military body. This is not a radical idea. Several Frontier Settlements run exactly this model for atmospheric launch corridors. The Kessel Collective has had one since 2918 and it turns a small surplus every year, which they reinvest in range maintenance.

The schematics for Kessel's governance model are public. I've linked them in the archive. Anyone can read them. Anyone can copy them.

The SDC's offer isn't nothing. A launch corridor is a launch corridor. Small operators who need slots and can tolerate the coordination overhead should evaluate it on its practical merits.

But let's not call it community access. Let's call it what it is: a landlord with a vacancy, offering a lease with conditions, in a building where they still control every door.
