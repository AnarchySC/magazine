# No One Has Ever Gone Broke Underestimating Galactic Intelligence: New Evidence Submitted

Let me tell you about the best sales pitch I ever heard.

I was moving comm subscriptions in the Belt, back when the Ceres relays were still patched together with splice-wire and optimism. My floor manager — a beautiful crook named Vasquez — had a technique he called the *Fog Wall*. You didn't lie, exactly. You just built a sentence so long, so stuffed with technical-sounding syllables, that by the time the mark reached the end of it, they'd forgotten what the question was. He sold 40,000 subscriptions in a quarter. Retired to Ganymede. Sends me a card every solstice.

I thought about Vasquez the moment I read Resolution 7-Theta-44.

The Interstellar Assembly — our galaxy's most prestigious collection of individuals who have never built, hauled, welded, or grown anything — passed it last cycle with a 94% approval margin. It is titled, and I am not fabricating this: *The Galactic Transparency and Citizen Empowerment Through Participatory Deliberative Alignment Framework (Phase One).*

Phase One.

Here's the thing about a title like that — it is not an accident. Every word in it was chosen by someone who understood that language, deployed correctly, functions as a sedative. *Transparency* tells you they're being honest. *Empowerment* tells you you're gaining something. *Participatory Deliberative Alignment* tells you absolutely nothing while sounding like it tells you everything. *Framework* whispers: don't worry, the details are handled.

The details, when you find them on page 340 of the resolution's appendices — filed, I'll note, at 0300 station time on a Foundersday weekend — establish a new bureau, the Office of Galactic Civic Coherence, staffed by 4,200 administrators, funded at 1.1 trillion SGC annually, tasked with *studying* citizen engagement metrics across all Colony Stations and Frontier Settlements.

Studying.

One trillion, one hundred billion Standard Galactic Credits. To study.

Look, I hauled ice on a three-man rig for four years. If I told my crew I needed a trillion credits to *study* the problem of ice, they would have left me in a storage bay and filed the paperwork on the way home. But here we are.

Mencken — old Earth writer, the kind who got thrown out of polite society for being correct too often — wrote somewhere that the whole aim of practical politics is to keep the populace alarmed by an endless series of hobgoblins, most of them imaginary. What he didn't live long enough to see is that you don't even need the hobgoblin anymore. The resolution *itself* is the hobgoblin. Dense enough to frighten, vague enough to mean anything, official enough to silence the person asking questions in the back of the room.

That's not a bug. That's the design.

The real question is never what a resolution says. It's what it *does*. Resolution 7-Theta-44 centralizes oversight of colonial communication networks under a single appointed director, who reports to a subcommittee, which reports to a council, which reports to the Assembly floor — which we have already established votes 94% on whatever lands in front of it before lunch.

Same rules for everyone. If I built a comm network that routed through seven management layers before a signal reached its destination, the engineers would mutiny. The Assembly calls this *governance*.

Who's trying to control who here? Follow the org chart down far enough and you find the answer. It's always the same answer.

Vasquez used to say: the marks don't need to understand the product. They need to feel like they do.

The Assembly read that man's mind across nine hundred years.

I'm not angry. I want to be clear about that. Anger at the Fog Wall is like being angry at weather. What I am is precise. Precise about what this is, precise about who benefits, and precise about the fact that the 94% who voted yes knew — at some functional, professional level — that their constituents would not read page 340.

They were correct.

They are always correct about that.

And yet: here you are, reading this. Which means something.

Maybe not enough. But something.
