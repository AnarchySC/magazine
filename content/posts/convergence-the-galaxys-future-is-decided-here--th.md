# CONVERGENCE: The Galaxy's Future Is Decided Here — The Uninvited Don't Exist

Somewhere between the third moon of Jupiter and a heavily encrypted invitation list, the people who already own everything are meeting to decide who gets to own the rest.

The club is called **Convergence**. You've never heard of it. That's not an accident.

---

Volkov Kai is not a household name. He prefers it that way. Former chairman of **Nexus Processing**, current majority stakeholder of four of the nine largest fabrication networks in the Core Systems, architect of the Orion Trust's 2931 restructuring that quietly absorbed eleven independent shipyards — Kai doesn't need your attention. He already has your supply chain.

For the past six years, Kai has hosted **Convergence**: a biannual gathering at his private orbital station above Callisto, attendance by invitation only, agenda unpublished, Neural-net access suspended for the duration. Guests arrive on unmarked transports. Guest lists are never released.

We know who attends because three of them talked. Anonymously. Because they're terrified.

---

The attendee profile is consistent: **mega-corp principals, GCB advisory board members, two sitting Interstellar Assembly faction leaders, three senior Solar Defense Compact procurement officers, and — this is the part that should make you stop chewing — at least four individuals currently under active Terran Intelligence Bureau investigation for separate matters.**

Different files. Same room.

I don't deal in intentions. I deal in actions. And the action here is: men and women who regulate each other's industries, who audit each other's contracts, who are legally prohibited from coordinating on policy — are spending seventy-two hours together on a station with no external comms, eating the same food, sleeping in adjacent cabins, and calling it a "philosophical dialogue."

That's not a dialogue. That's a board meeting without minutes.

---

Kai's stated purpose, in the one interview he gave to **Earth Network News** in 2932 — an interview so soft it practically needed a pillow — was to "bring together the galaxy's most consequential minds to discuss civilizational trajectories."

You said the opposite in 2931. Which version is lying?

In 2931, a leaked internal memo from Nexus Processing — authenticated, never disputed — described Convergence as a venue for "aligning stakeholder positions ahead of Assembly cycles." Aligning stakeholder positions. Before votes. Before the public knows what's being voted on.

He hasn't explained the discrepancy. ENN hasn't asked.

---

The topics, according to our sources, are not abstract.

Convergence 2933 reportedly included sessions on: **GCB prime lending rate scheduling for the following fiscal year**. **Frontier Settlement resource extraction licensing frameworks**. **The proposed Neural-net identification mandate** — which became Assembly Resolution 7741 eight months later, almost word for word.

Almost. Word. For. Word.

I asked the Assembly's lead sponsor of Resolution 7741, Delegate Asha Venn, whether she attended Convergence 2933. Her communications director told me the delegate had "no recollection of any such event." I sent the transport manifest. The communications director stopped answering.

That's not an answer. Try again.

---

Here is what a private club of this design actually is, stripped of the vocabulary they prefer:

It is a **pre-legislative coordination mechanism** for people who do not need to win elections and are not subject to the transparency requirements that nominally bind the officials they're meeting with. It is a room where the price of your colony's power grid, the terms of your fabrication license, the scope of your Neural-net privacy, and the threshold of your children's debt ceiling are discussed — and you were not invited, and you will not be told what was decided, and the people who decided it will describe themselves as visionaries.

Contradictions don't exist. Check your premises.

---

Volkov Kai did not respond to fourteen separate requests for comment over six weeks. His legal team sent a single reply: *"Mr. Kai is a private citizen engaged in lawful private association. There is nothing further to say."*

They're right that it's lawful. That's the problem.

The galaxy spent two centuries designing oversight mechanisms for governments. It forgot to wonder what happens when governance moves somewhere without windows.

Convergence has been running for six years.

Ask yourself what's already been built.
