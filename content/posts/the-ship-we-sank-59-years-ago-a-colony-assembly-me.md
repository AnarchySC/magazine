# The Ship We Sank 59 Years Ago: A Colony Assembly Member Finally Demands an Inquiry

**陳 マックスウェル | Military & Security Correspondent**

---

In the year 2876, a Core Systems Communications Vessel — the *ECS Meridian* — was sailing routine patrol in the Cygnus Corridor when a Solar Defense Compact ally opened fire on it. For six hours.

They napalmed the deck. Then they machine-gunned the lifeboats.

Thirty-four fleet personnel died. One hundred and seventy-one were wounded. The *Meridian* was clearly marked. Its transponder was active. It was broadcasting Core Systems identification codes that any allied vessel would have recognized from across a star system.

The official finding: *Tragic accident. Misidentification. Everyone feels terrible.*

And then — and here's where it gets interesting — the Earth Unified Council *agreed with that*. Immediately. Enthusiastically. With a speed that should have raised at least one eyebrow somewhere in the Interstellar Assembly.

Wait, it gets better.

For fifty-nine years, survivors of the *Meridian* have been asking for a formal inquiry. Fifty-nine years of petitions, formal requests, neural transmissions to Assembly offices, and the kind of polite persistence that slowly breaks a person's soul. The Council's official position has remained, essentially: *We looked into it. Accident. Moving on.*

They said this. Then they moved on. I'll wait.

---

This week, Assembly Member Rael Okonkwo — representing Frontier Settlement Block 7, which is apparently the only place left where someone is willing to say an obvious thing out loud — formally introduced Resolution 2935-M114, demanding a full declassification of all Terran Intelligence Bureau files related to the *Meridian* incident and a new independent inquiry.

His statement to the Assembly floor included this remarkable sentence:

*"The fleet personnel who survived were ordered not to discuss the attack. Their commanding officers were reassigned. The after-action reports were classified above Top Stellar for three decades. This is not the behavior of an organization that believes it witnessed an accident."*

And nobody laughed?

The Assembly chamber, by all accounts, received this with the energy of a room that has been asked to smell something it has collectively decided does not smell.

---

I'm not saying the Solar Defense Compact deliberately destroyed a Core Systems vessel while the Council looked the other way to preserve a strategically convenient alliance. I'm just reading the incident report aloud.

The *Meridian* was a signals intelligence ship. It had been collecting communications in the Cygnus Corridor for eleven days prior to the attack. Whatever it had collected, it never transmitted home.

The TIB files remain classified.

The alliance remains intact.

The memorial to the thirty-four dead fleet personnel on Luna Station has a small plaque that describes them as victims of *"a wartime communications failure."* No names of the responsible party. No flag. Just *communications failure*, which is the bureaucratic equivalent of a shrug rendered in bronze.

Anyway.

---

Okonkwo's resolution will be assigned to the Assembly Defense Oversight Subcommittee, which has a backlog of approximately 340 unresolved inquiries and meets quarterly when quorum permits.

The Solar Defense Compact released a statement this morning. It was four sentences. The word *ally* appeared three times.

I just think it's funny — fifty-nine years later, the most dangerous thing in that chamber wasn't an accusation. It was a man reading a timeline out loud.

The survivors, most of them now quite elderly, are still asking.

The TIB files are still classified.

The alliance is fine.
