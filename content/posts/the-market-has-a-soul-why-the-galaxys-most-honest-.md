# The Market Has a Soul: Why the Galaxy's Most Honest Economics Textbook Is Also a Spiritual One

Alright, let me break this down—

Somewhere between the Ceres Exchange's seventeenth consecutive quarter of synthetic liquidity injections and the GCB's latest 'stabilization communiqué' — which, for the record, stabilized absolutely nothing except the net worth of Stellar Financial's board members — a small academic press on Kepler Station quietly released a book that should not exist.

It's called *Foundations of Economics: A Cosmic View.* Dr. Shen Ritenū, professor emeritus at the Free Academy of Callisto, wrote it. Nobody at Earth Network News has reviewed it. Funny, that.

I read it in one sitting. My tea went cold. I didn't care.

---

**HERE'S the beautiful part—**

Ritenū doesn't begin with supply curves. He doesn't begin with interest rate models or productivity indices. He begins with a question that no standard economics curriculum in the Core Systems will touch with a ten-meter docking pole:

*What are humans actually for?*

Because here's the thing. Every economics framework contains a hidden answer to that question. It just doesn't advertise it.

The GCB's framework says, implicitly: humans are economic units whose behavior must be managed, smoothed, and corrected by their betters. The Interstellar Assembly's framework says: humans are problems to be solved through allocation committees.

Ritenū says something different. He grounds his entire economic model in the observation that sentient beings are meaning-seeking creatures with genuine moral agency — and that this fact *changes everything* about how markets work and why they matter.

---

**Now watch what happens next...**

Here's where the whiteboard moment arrives. Draw two columns.

Column A: Standard Galactic Credit-based macroeconomics as taught at the Earth Unified Academy. Aggregate demand. Credit multipliers. 'Optimal' lending rates determined by nine unelected officials in a climate-controlled room on orbital platform seven.

Column B: Ritenū's framework. Individual humans make choices. Those choices are *subjective* — meaning you value a warm meal differently than I do, and no central authority can calculate the 'correct' value for either of us. Markets are simply the *visible record* of those billions of individual value judgments, continuously updated, constantly discovering information that no committee could ever possess.

You see what he did there?

Column A requires a controller. Column B makes a controller not just unnecessary but *mathematically impossible to justify.*

This is the part they don't want you to understand.

---

**The Spiritual Thread**

Now here's where skeptics will squirm, and I want to address that directly.

Ritenū is writing from a specific cosmological-spiritual tradition — one of the old Earth philosophical lineages that survived the Great Diaspora. Some readers will share it. Many won't.

It doesn't matter.

Because what Ritenū is doing philosophically — grounding economic rights in the *intrinsic dignity of the choosing agent* — that argument works whether your metaphysics comes from ancient Earth scripture, Outer Rim Stoic tradition, or pure secular humanism.

The conclusion is the same: if a person is genuinely a moral agent with real choices and real consequences, then a system that *hijacks those choices through credit dilution, regulatory capture, and manufactured dependency* is not just economically inefficient.

It is wrong.

And THAT'S the Cantillon Effect, baby — dressed not in charts and indices, but in language that asks you to look at the person being robbed, not just the mechanism of the robbery.

---

**Why It Matters Right Now**

The GCB just announced its fourth consecutive quarter of 'adaptive credit expansion.' Stellar Financial's share price is up. Frontier Settlement median purchasing power is down for the eleventh year running.

Somebody is getting the new credits first. Somebody is getting them last. That gap — *that specific, predictable, geometric gap* — is not a glitch. It's the product.

Ritenū's book gives you the language to say so with moral clarity, not just statistical frustration.

Get it before someone quietly pulls it from the distribution nodes.
