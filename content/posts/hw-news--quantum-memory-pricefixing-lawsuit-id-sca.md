## This Week in Hardware: Four Stories, One Uncomfortable Pattern

Let's run through it. Four stories this week, each one worth your attention for different reasons. As always, methodology and source links at the bottom — check my work.

---

### 1. Quantum Memory Price-Fixing Lawsuit: Three Corps, One Price

A coalition of independent fabrication operators has filed suit against HeliosChip, NovaMem, and Orion Quantum Storage — the three manufacturers who together control roughly 91% of galactic quantum memory production — alleging coordinated price manipulation spanning nearly a decade.

The core claim: internal communications obtained through the discovery process show executives at all three companies exchanging production volume targets in what plaintiffs call 'supply theater' — deliberately manufacturing artificial shortages to keep prices elevated across the entire market simultaneously.

The interesting part isn't that this allegedly happened. It's *why* it was allegedly so easy. When three entities control 91% of anything, coordinating behavior becomes structurally simple even without explicit agreements. The market shape itself does the work.

Here's how you can try this yourself: pull the historical quantum memory pricing data from the Ceres Exchange public ledger. Plot it against announced production volumes from all three corps over the same period. The correlation is... instructive. I did this last month and I'm still thinking about it.

Trial date is set for early 2937. Don't hold your breath.

### 2. ID Verification Mandates: Creeping Quietly Through Colony Administrations

Seven additional Colony Administrations this week adopted 'voluntary' neural-net identity verification frameworks — the same architecture we covered three months ago when the first wave of mandates emerged. The word 'voluntary' continues to do extraordinary heavy lifting in these documents.

The practical effect: any operator running a distributed processing node, a public fabrication terminal, or a citizen-accessible data relay must now log verified identity for all users, or face compliance penalties.

Note what this is not: it is not a law passed by any deliberative body. It's a framework adopted by Colony Administration regulators, mostly in overnight announcements, citing 'security harmonization' language that traces back to a single Terran Intelligence Bureau advisory document from 2933.

They patented *math*. Think about that. Now they're licensing the architecture for knowing who you are.

Full schematic of what these verification nodes actually collect — versus what they claim to collect — is in my public lab archive. The gap is wide.

### 3. Interstellar Assembly Wants 5% of VoidCore's AI Division

This one is genuinely difficult to parse without laughing.

The Interstellar Assembly's Commerce Subcommittee has formally proposed — in writing, in a real document — that VoidCore AI Systems transfer a 5% equity stake to the Assembly as a condition of continued operating licenses in Core Systems territories.

The stated justification: VoidCore AI was 'substantially developed using publicly funded research infrastructure.' Which is true. That research was also freely published, freely cited, and freely used by approximately eight hundred other organizations who are not being asked for equity stakes.

The Assembly's counterargument appears to be that VoidCore got bigger than the others. I don't understand the question. Why wouldn't you share the research? The interesting part isn't the audacity — it's that the Assembly seems genuinely confused about the difference between 'we funded open research' and 'we own what came from it.'

VoidCore has not commented publicly. Sources familiar with the negotiation suggest the number will land closer to 2%. Everyone walks away feeling something.

### 4. NebulaPlay Drops Physical Cartridges for the NP-6 Platform

And finally: NebulaPlay has confirmed the NP-6 game pod, expected late 2936, will ship with no physical cartridge slot. Games will be available exclusively through the NebulaPlay distributed processing core — their storefront, their servers, their terms.

The company says this is 'streamlining the player experience.' What it actually streamlines is NebulaPlay's ability to revoke, modify, or reprice anything you think you own at any moment of their choosing.

The right-to-fabricate angle here is real: without physical media, there is no community-maintained archive, no independent repair ecosystem for software, no ability to run a game after NebulaPlay decides to sunset it. These are not hypothetical concerns. NebulaPlay sunset three entire platform libraries in the last decade.

For what it's worth: open-format physical cartridge readers compatible with legacy NebulaPlay media are fully schematics-available through my lab. If you have a collection, now is a reasonable time to think about preservation.
