# The Stadium and the Street: Why Core Systems Fans Watch Sport While Frontier Fans Live It

*by ソフィア 中村, Human Interest & Lifestyle Editor*

---

The first thing I noticed at Olympia Arena on Earth Station was how quiet everyone was.

Not quiet like watching something sacred. Quiet like watching something that was happening *to* them. Fifty thousand people in color-coordinated seats, drinking beverages sold to them by a subsidiary of the team's parent mega-corp, wearing jerseys licensed by the same. When the Terran Stormers scored, a sound cue triggered in the ceiling. The crowd cheered *after* the sound told them to.

I wrote in my notebook: *Who is this for?*

---

Three weeks later I was in Docking Ring 7 at Kepler-7 Colony for what a local dockworker named Tomas described as "the game." There was no arena. There was a loading bay with the cargo pushed to one side, a painted line on the durasteel floor, and approximately four hundred people who had organized themselves into something loud and alive before I even found a place to stand.

No assigned seats. No licensing. No sound cue.

When a player scored — a woman named Devi who welds hull plates twelve hours a day — the crowd did not cheer. It *erupted*. People grabbed each other. A man I'd never met grabbed my arm. I grabbed back. I didn't know the rules. I didn't need to.

I asked Tomas who owned the team.

He looked at me the way people look at you when you've said something that doesn't quite parse. "Owns it?" he said. "We all play on it."

Back home, we'd just call that sport.

---

The distinction, I've come to think, is not really about sport at all.

In the Core Systems, entertainment — including athletic competition — has been processed through the same机能 (kinou, machinery) that processes everything else: monetized, packaged, delivered to passive recipients who pay for the privilege of feeling something they've been told to feel. The Ceres Exchange lists seventeen publicly traded sports mega-corps. The Terran Athletics Commission has 340 full-time administrators. Fan behavior is regulated. Ticket prices ensure the "right" audience.

Franchise sport, I realized, is just hierarchy in shorts.

In the Frontier Settlements, sport survived differently. It survived because nobody came to organize it, monetize it, or protect it from itself. It survived as what sport apparently used to be: an excuse for neighbors to stand close together and care intensely about the same thing at the same time.

They figured it out together. Nobody told them to.

---

I sat with Devi after the game. She poured tea while she explained — in the unhurried way of someone who has never been asked to justify something obvious — that the game had been running for forty years in this docking ring. Teams change. Rules get argued over every season. Last year they voted to add a new scoring zone and it caused a three-week dispute that she describes as "honestly more fun than the actual games."

No commissioner intervened. No franchise agreement protected legacy teams. No corporate partner had opinions.

"Sometimes we get it wrong," she said. "Then we fix it next year."

I asked if there were ever fights.

"Of course," she said. "But never about the game. Only ever about the rules. Which is different."

This is what it actually looks like when neighbors trust each other.

---

Back at Olympia Arena, I'd watched a man get escorted out by security for standing up during play. Standing. He'd stood up because something thrilling happened and his body responded the way bodies respond to thrilling things. He was removed for it.

The sport continued. The sound cue played. Fifty thousand people cheered on schedule.

I asked who was in charge here. The security officer looked at me strangely.

"Management," he said, as though that explained anything.

---

Sport, at its origin, is one of the oldest technologies humans ever built for the same purpose as every other community practice that actually works: it makes strangers into neighbors. It gives people a reason to stand next to each other and feel the same thing. The Frontier retained this. The Core Systems sold it, and received in return a product that looks like sport from a distance but performs a different function entirely.

The product is not community. The product is the feeling of community, delivered on schedule, at a price point, with the chaos removed.

漠然とした孤独 — bakuzen to shita kodoku, a diffuse, formless loneliness — is what I felt in that arena. Surrounded by people. Completely alone.

Devi's docking bay smelled like machine oil and someone's reheated dinner. I have thought about it every day since.
