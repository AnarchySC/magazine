# Question the Definition of 'Rich': Options Are the Only Wealth That Matters

Every few cycles, the neural feeds produce a new prophet in miniature. Thirty seconds. A confident voice. A reframe so clean it feels like a window being opened in a sealed chamber.

This season's version goes like this: *Being rich isn't about fancy stuff. It's about having options.*

The clip runs thirty-two seconds. It has been shared 4.7 billion times across the neural feeds. Someone in a well-lit habitat somewhere is nodding. Someone on a Frontier Settlement bunk is watching it for the third time, hoping it'll stick.

But what is it *actually* saying?

---

The surface story is about redefining wealth. The real story is about who benefits when you internalize that definition.

The argument is not wrong. Options — the freedom to refuse bad work, to move between stations, to say *no* to the contract that treats you like equipment — are genuinely more meaningful than another piece of Ceres Exchange merchandise. Anyone who has been trapped in a labor bond or a colony subscription debt already knows this in their body, not just their mobi feed.

So yes. True.

But look at what the ending asks us to accept.

The clip never mentions how you *get* options. It treats optionality as a mindset — something you choose to cultivate, like a hydroponic herb garden in a zero-G apartment. *You weren't thinking about it correctly. Now you are. You're rich.*

This is philosophy as anesthesia.

---

Options are not a philosophy. They are a *resource*. Like quantum fuel, like fabrication credits, like breathable atmosphere — options have a supply chain. They come from somewhere. They are produced, hoarded, rationed, and — most importantly — *denied*.

A Frontier Settlement colonist working a processing contract with a non-compete clause embedded in their housing agreement does not lack options because they haven't watched the right neural feed. They lack options because someone upstream in the Standard Galactic Credit system made a calculation that their scarcity was *profitable*.

The wealthy have always been comfortable with the word *freedom*. What they are less comfortable with is the question: *free from what, exactly? And who built the cage?*

---

I have watched this clip — and its ten thousand ancestors across the decades — and I notice the villain is always the same: your *own thinking*. Not the GCB prime lending rate. Not the mega-corp housing covenant. Not the Interstellar Assembly's labor classification codes that define half the galaxy as 'independent contractors' to avoid benefit obligations.

Your *mindset*.

This is a story about what it means to live under a system so total that it has learned to narrate your imprisonment as a *personal development opportunity*.

The thirty-second prophet is not lying. Options *are* the real wealth. But the clip stops precisely where philosophy should begin: *who controls the supply of options, and what would it take to change that?*

That question doesn't fit in thirty seconds.

That question doesn't get shared 4.7 billion times.

And that, I think, is exactly the point.
