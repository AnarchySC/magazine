# Sugar in the Signal: A Critical Look at ENN's Evening Broadcast

I used to haul ice on the Ganymede-Ceres run. Twelve-hour shifts. Nothing but the hum of the cryo-pumps and whatever was piping through the station feed when I docked. Back then, ENN felt like information. Sparse. A little cold. But it told you what a cargo rate was, where the storms were, who'd gone dark in the Belt.

Somebody at Earth Network News decided that era was over.

Last week they debuted *Horizon Standard*, their new three-hour primetime block. High production. Gorgeous. The kind of lighting that makes everybody look like they're about to receive a humanitarian award or confess to something spiritual. I watched it six nights running. Not out of masochism — well, not entirely — but because someone has to take notes.

Here's the thing about *Horizon Standard* —

It is technically a news program the way a thermal pod is technically a sandwich bag. Correct category. Completely wrong purpose.

The first eighteen minutes of Tuesday's broadcast were devoted to what anchors Lena Voss and Soreya Mak called a "galactic mood calibration segment." This involved soft synthesizer music, rotating holographic nebulae, and Voss reading what I can only describe as an affirmational prose poem about "the resilience of sentient life in uncertain times." No dateline. No sourcing. No event prompting any of this. Just vibes, professionally delivered at broadcast-quality bitrate across sixteen star systems.

Look, I'm not saying feelings don't matter. I'm saying that when the Ceres Exchange dropped eleven percent in forty minutes last Thursday — which it did, I was watching the feeds — *Horizon Standard* spent that same forty minutes on a profile of a sculptor from Europa who makes abstract pieces representing "the emotional weight of colonial memory." Beautiful story, probably. Wrong hour.

The Mencken line comes back to me every time I sit through one of these blocks. The one about democracy — how the common people know what they want and deserve to get it good and hard. ENN has run the logic further. They've decided the common people don't want news at all. They want the *feeling* of being informed without the metabolic cost of actual information. So that's the product now. Nutritional-label news. Serving size: one segment. Calories: zero. Emotional satisfaction: carefully engineered.

The real question is whether this is incompetence or design.

I lean design. You don't spend what ENN spent on the *Horizon Standard* set — those curved panels have to run forty thousand SGC per unit — to accidentally create something empty. That's not a bug. That's the design.

They've done the research. They know that colony workers on twelve-hour rotations will fall asleep to this. They know that the rhythm of a well-modulated anchor voice, against that specific blue-gray palette, produces something neurologically close to being told everything is fine. It's not information. It's a sedative with a chyron.

The saddest segment I watched was a panel — three experts, excellent credentials, the kind of people who've actually done things — discussing a potential breakdown in rare-element distribution contracts between Frontier Settlements and Core Systems manufacturers. Real stakes. Real complexity. Real consequences for maybe forty million people in outer stations.

They got four minutes and twenty seconds.

The sculptor from Europa got fourteen.

I don't blame Voss or Mak. They're professionals working inside a format they didn't build. I don't even blame the executives who built it — they're responding to signal, same as anyone. I blame the part of the audience that decided, somewhere along the way, that comfort and information were the same thing.

They're not. Never were.

Same rules for everyone. Including the rule that says you don't get to understand the galaxy by watching someone describe their feelings about it.

Ice doesn't move itself. Neither does truth.
