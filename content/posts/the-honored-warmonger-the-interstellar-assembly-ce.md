# The Honored Warmonger: The Interstellar Assembly Celebrates Its Most Enthusiastic Advocate for Killing

*By 宗像 レイナ, Galactic Affairs Correspondent*

---

Cassius Graham-Voss has never deployed with a fleet. He has, however, voted to send other people's children to thirty-seven distinct military engagements over the course of a thirty-year Assembly career.

This week, his colleagues are honoring him for it.

The commendation ceremony, scheduled for the main rotunda of the Assembly's Titan Annex, will formally recognize Graham-Voss for his — and I am quoting the official citation here — *"tireless commitment to galactic stability and the principled use of Solar Defense Compact resources in service of interstellar peace."*

I'll let that sentence breathe for a moment.

Graham-Voss, for those who have managed the feat of not knowing him, is the Assembly's most reliable vote for military spending increases, fleet deployment authorizations, and pre-emptive "security operations" in Frontier Settlements that had the poor judgment to sit on rare elements. He is what political speechwriters — and I used to be one, so I know the taxonomy — call an *asset*. Reliable. Predictable. Never asks whether a war is necessary, only whether the contractors are ready.

In 2924, he stood at this same podium and called for "overwhelming and sustained force" against the Kessel Corridor settlements. The settlements were, at the time, in an unresolved trade arbitration. Not a military conflict. An arbitration. Graham-Voss wanted fleet carriers. The Corridor got fleet carriers.

Three thousand people were displaced.

He voted yes on the Proxima authorization. Yes on the Callisto perimeter expansion. Yes on every supplemental defense appropriation that passed his desk, including the three that were later flagged by the Assembly's own audit bureau as containing contractor overcharges totaling approximately 400 billion Standard Galactic Credits.

When asked about the audit findings in 2931, he said the overcharges were "operational complexities inherent to rapid deployment scenarios."

I don't deal in intentions. I deal in actions. The action was: he signed. The action was: he voted. The action was: he kept voting, for thirty years, every time someone with a Solar Defense Compact contract needed an Assembly majority.

Now he's being honored.

The ceremony will feature remarks from four current Assembly members, a recorded tribute from the Earth Unified Council, and — this is not a fabrication — a scale model of a fleet carrier that Graham-Voss "personally advocated for" during the 2928 Naval Expansion Act, presented as a commemorative gift.

A model of a weapon. As a gift. For a man who never went near one.

I asked the Assembly's communications bureau whether any surviving families from the Kessel Corridor displacement were invited to the ceremony.

They sent me a seating chart.

There's a phrase that gets used in these rooms: *he served with distinction.* I spent fifteen years watching that phrase get applied to people who served no one but the institutions that fed them. Graham-Voss didn't serve the Frontier Settlements he voted to bomb. He didn't serve the fleet personnel he deployed. He served the system that handed him a seat, and the contractors who kept that seat comfortable.

The Interstellar Assembly is giving him a plaque for it.

Contradictions don't exist. Check your premises.

The premises here are very simple: an institution that authorizes killing as a policy instrument will, eventually, celebrate the men who made that authorization easy. This is not an anomaly. It is the natural end state of any body that has mistaken the power to deploy force for the wisdom to avoid it.

Graham-Voss is not the disease. He is the symptom with a commemorative model starship and a rotunda full of applause.
