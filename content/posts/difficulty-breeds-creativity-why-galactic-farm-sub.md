# Difficulty Breeds Creativity: Why Galactic Farm Subsidies Are Killing Us

*by 松田 ジェイド*

---

Rael Santine grows food on a rotating centrifuge module in the Kessel Corridor with no GCB backing, no Earth Council 'Harvest Stabilization' credits, and no synthetic agrochem contracts with Stellar Agronomics Corp. He also, by every metric that actually matters, grows *better food* than anyone in the Core Systems subsidy network.

He has a theory about why.

"The bailout is the poison," he told me over a meal of something he'd grown himself — a dense, mineral-tasting root vegetable I couldn't name but couldn't stop eating. "When you know the Council will cover your losses, you stop *thinking*. You stop *adapting*. You just repeat last cycle's failure at a larger scale and wait for the credit transfer."

But what is it actually saying — this argument Santine has been making for thirty years on the frontier circuit, in pamphlets distributed on Independent transmissions, at agro-forums where he is alternately celebrated and thrown out?

The surface story is about farming subsidies. The real story is about what difficulty *is for*.

---

Santine's philosophy sits squarely in the regenerative tradition — the belief that living systems, including human economic systems, require stress to evolve. A plant with no drought develops shallow roots. A farmer with no risk develops shallow thinking.

The Earth Unified Council's Harvest Stabilization Program — now in its 340th consecutive year of 'temporary emergency support' — guarantees minimum credit returns to any agro-operator producing within approved parameters. Approved parameters, of course, means approved *methods*: the soil-chemistry cocktails from Stellar Agronomics, the approved seed libraries from CosmicSeed Corp, the industrial scale that makes subsidy distribution administratively convenient.

Creativity is not in the approved parameters.

"Every time a synthetic agrochem kills a soil microbiome and the crop fails, the Council pays the farmer not to think about what he did wrong," Santine said. "He doesn't have to solve the problem. The problem is *bought off*. And then he does it again."

Look at what the ending asks us to accept: three centuries of 'emergency' credits, and Core Systems food quality has declined in every measurable dimension. Nutrient density. Flavor. Ecological resilience. The subsidized farms got *larger* and the food got *worse*.

The frontier operator, living and dying on her own merit, invented closed-loop composting systems, mycorrhizal acceleration techniques, multi-axis rotation gravity farming. She had to. Nobody was coming to save her.

---

This is a story about what it means to *need the answer yourself*.

Santine doesn't romanticize suffering. He's precise about it. "I'm not saying hardship is good. I'm saying the *removal of consequence* is catastrophic. There's a difference between a safety net and a cage."

The villain here — and Santine names it clearly — is not the farmer who takes the subsidy. It's the system that decided farmers were too important to be allowed to fail, which translated, in practice, into: too important to be allowed to *learn*.

The Council believes — and the Harvest Stabilization Program agrees more than it admits — that human beings cannot be trusted with their own survival. That the correct response to a failed harvest is a credit transfer, not a question. That stability is more valuable than the knowledge that can only be earned through instability.

Santine believes the opposite. And three decades of better food, grown on centrifuge rock with no institutional backing, is the argument he keeps submitting as evidence.

I ate a second serving of that root vegetable.

I didn't ask what it was. I just knew it was *real*.
